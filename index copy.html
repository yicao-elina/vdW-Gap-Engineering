A Universal Framework for van der Waals Gap Engineering

Harnessing single atoms to rewrite the physical and electronic rules of 2D materials.

Yi | PhD Candidate | Johns Hopkins University

Explore the Research

A New Paradigm for Materials by Design

The properties of two-dimensional (2D) materials are fundamentally dictated by the fragile van der Waals (vdW) gap between their layers. Current tuning methods, such as chemical alloying, often introduce disorder, compromising performance. We introduce a clean, precise, and universal framework to engineer this critical dimension by intercalating individual atoms.

Our research establishes two distinct and controllable regimes: Covalent Stapling, where transition metals form strong directional bonds to contract the vdW gap and mechanically stiffen the material, and Ionic Spacing, where large, electropositive atoms expand the gap through electrostatic and Pauli repulsion.

By combining first-principles Density Functional Theory (DFT) with state-of-the-art Graph Neural Networks, we have developed a predictive model that maps this vast design space. This work culminates in a novel application: using engineered interlayer atoms as active sites for ultra-sensitive single-molecule sensors, capable of transducing local chemical events into clear electronic signals.

Two Fundamental Regimes of Atomic Control

1. Covalent Stapling

Transition metals like Chromium (Cr) and Vanadium (V) position themselves at the center of the vdW gap and form strong, directional d-p orbital hybridizations with chalcogen atoms in the host layers. This covalent interaction acts as an atomic "staple," pulling the layers together, contracting the vdW gap by up to 10%, and significantly increasing the interlayer mechanical stiffness.

2. Ionic / Steric Spacing

Large, electropositive atoms like Cesium (Cs) and Barium (Ba) readily donate their valence electrons to the host layers. The resulting ionic repulsion, combined with Pauli repulsion from the large atomic core, forces the layers apart. This "spacing" effect expands the vdW gap by over 15% and mechanically softens the material, creating a tunable nano-cushion.

From First-Principles Data to a Universal Design Map

To move beyond a descriptive study, we trained a machine learning model on a comprehensive DFT dataset spanning multiple hosts (Sb₂Te₃, MoS₂, InSe) and dozens of intercalants. The model learns the underlying physics to predict the outcome for any atom in any layered host.

Figure: The universal design map generated by our ML model. By inputting fundamental atomic properties, we can predict whether an element will act as a "staple" (blue regions) or a "spacer" (red regions) without the need for expensive DFT calculations. This predictive power accelerates the discovery of materials with tailored interlayer properties.

Application Spotlight: Single-Atom Interlayer Sensors

This framework is not merely a theoretical curiosity. We demonstrate that the unique electronic environment created by a covalent "staple" can be harnessed to create a new class of chemical sensors with single-atom sensitivity.

An Atomic-Scale Transducer

The covalent bond of an intercalated Cr atom acts as an atomic-scale transducer. When a target analyte like NO₂ adsorbs nearby, it perturbs this sensitive electronic state.

Our DFT calculations show this binding event creates a distinct mid-gap state—a clear, unambiguous electronic signature. This mechanism allows a local chemical event to be translated into a measurable electrical signal, paving the way for ultra-selective sensors built from atomically-engineered 2D materials.
